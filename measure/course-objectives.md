# Integrated Math 3 Course Objectives

This document extracts course objectives from the `Today's Goal` or `Today's Goals` sections in the lesson source files under `curriculum/modules/module-*-lesson-*`. Each objective is labeled by module number and letter, and each one is followed by a concrete description of proficiency.

## Module 1: Quadratic Functions and Complex Numbers

**1a. Graph quadratic functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph a quadratic from an equation, table, or context; identify the vertex, axis of symmetry, intercepts, and direction of opening; and use those features to describe the parabola.

**1b. Find and interpret the average rate of change.**  
Proficiency means a student can compute average rate of change from an equation, table, graph, or model and explain what the resulting rate means in context.

**1c. Solve quadratic equations by graphing.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph a quadratic equation or compare two graphs, identify the relevant x-values, and state the real solutions correctly.

**1d. Interpret solutions (roots) in context.**  
Proficiency means a student can explain what roots represent in a modeled situation, decide whether a root is reasonable in context, and communicate the meaning of zeroes clearly.

**1e. Understand and use the imaginary unit `i`.**  
Proficiency means a student can rewrite square roots of negative numbers using `i`, apply `i^2 = -1`, and distinguish real, imaginary, and complex values.

**1f. Perform operations with complex numbers.**  
Proficiency means a student can add, subtract, and multiply complex numbers, simplify results into `a + bi` form, and match real and imaginary parts correctly.

**1g. Solve quadratic equations with imaginary solutions.**  
Proficiency means a student can solve quadratics whose discriminant is negative, express solutions using complex numbers, and justify why no real roots exist.

**1h. Solve quadratic equations by factoring.**  
Proficiency means a student can write a quadratic in standard form, factor it correctly, and use the factors to determine all solutions.

**1i. Use the Zero Product Property.**  
Proficiency means a student can apply the rule that if a product is zero then at least one factor is zero, and use that idea accurately to solve quadratic equations.

**1j. Recognize special factor patterns (GCF, trinomials, difference of squares).**  
Proficiency means a student can choose an appropriate factoring strategy, recognize common structures quickly, and factor expressions with minimal algebraic error.

**1k. Solve quadratic equations by completing the square.**  
Proficiency means a student can reorganize an equation, add the necessary term to both sides, solve the resulting perfect square equation, and produce exact solutions.

**1l. Rewrite quadratic expressions as perfect square trinomials.**  
Proficiency means a student can determine the term needed to complete the square and rewrite an expression in equivalent perfect-square form.

**1m. Connect completing the square to vertex form.**  
Proficiency means a student can convert a quadratic to vertex form and use that form to identify and interpret the vertex.

**1n. Use the Quadratic Formula to solve equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify `a`, `b`, and `c`, substitute accurately into the formula, simplify exact values, and report all solutions correctly.

**1o. Use the discriminant to determine the number and type of solutions.**  
Proficiency means a student can compute `b^2 - 4ac`, predict whether solutions are two real, one real, or two complex values, and connect that result to the graph.

**1p. Graph quadratic inequalities in two variables.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph the boundary parabola, choose solid or dashed boundaries correctly, test points, and shade the correct region.

**1q. Solve quadratic inequalities in one variable by graphing.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify where a parabola lies above or below the x-axis and express the solution set using inequalities or interval notation.

**1r. Interpret solutions in context.**  
Proficiency means a student can explain what an interval or shaded region means in a real situation and determine which values are reasonable or allowable.

**1s. Solve systems involving a linear and a quadratic equation.**  
Proficiency means a student can solve a linear-quadratic system by graphing, substitution, or elimination and determine all real intersection points.

**1t. Solve systems of two quadratic equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can solve a quadratic-quadratic system graphically or algebraically and determine all real intersection points.

**1u. Interpret solutions as points of intersection.**  
Proficiency means a student can explain what each intersection point means in context and relate the number of intersections to the number of real solutions.

## Module 2: Polynomials and Polynomial Functions

**2a. Graph and analyze power functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph basic power functions, compare even and odd power behavior, and describe symmetry and end behavior.

**2b. Graph and analyze polynomial functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can use degree, leading coefficient, intercepts, and overall shape to sketch and interpret polynomial graphs.

**2c. Approximate zeros by graphing polynomial functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify approximate x-intercepts from a graph and interpret them as zeros of the function.

**2d. Find extrema of polynomial functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify local maximum and minimum points from graphs and use them to describe how the function changes.

**2e. Add and subtract polynomials.**  
Proficiency means a student can combine like terms accurately, organize work by degree, and produce simplified polynomial results.

**2f. Multiply polynomials.**  
Proficiency means a student can multiply monomials and polynomials correctly, account for every term, and simplify the final expression.

**2g. Divide polynomials by using long division.**  
Proficiency means a student can set up polynomial long division correctly, complete the algorithm accurately, and express the result with quotient and remainder.

**2h. Divide polynomials by using synthetic division.**  
Proficiency means a student can use synthetic division when appropriate, interpret the output correctly, and relate it to polynomial division.

**2i. Expand powers of binomials by using Pascal's Triangle and the Binomial Theorem.**  
Proficiency means a student can generate correct coefficients, write all terms in order, and explain how exponents and coefficients follow binomial patterns.

## Module 3: Polynomial Equations

**3a. Solve polynomial equations by graphing.**  
Proficiency means a student can use a graph to locate real zeros, estimate solutions to a reasonable precision, and connect the graph to the equation.

**3b. Solve polynomial equations by factoring.**  
Proficiency means a student can factor polynomial expressions and use those factors to find all solutions that follow from the factorization.

**3c. Solve polynomial equations by writing them in quadratic form and factoring.**  
Proficiency means a student can recognize quadratic structure inside a higher-degree equation, substitute strategically, solve the resulting quadratic, and convert back to the original variable.

**3d. Prove polynomial identities and use them to describe numerical relationships.**  
Proficiency means a student can transform expressions algebraically to verify an identity and explain how the identity represents a valid numerical relationship.

**3e. Evaluate functions by using synthetic substitution.**  
Proficiency means a student can use synthetic substitution to evaluate a polynomial at a given value and interpret the result accurately.

**3f. Use the Factor Theorem to determine factors of polynomials.**  
Proficiency means a student can test candidate zeros efficiently and decide whether a linear expression is a factor based on the function value.

**3g. Use the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra to determine the number and type of roots of polynomial equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can state how many roots a polynomial must have, including complex roots, and use degree to reason about possible solution types.

**3h. Determine the number and type of roots of polynomial equations, find zeros, and use zeros to graph polynomial functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can combine algebraic and graphical evidence to find zeros, classify roots, and use those zeros to sketch polynomial graphs.

## Module 4: Inverses and Radical Functions

**4a. Find sums, differences, products, and quotients of functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can perform operations on functions correctly, write resulting expressions clearly, and state any domain restrictions.

**4b. Find compositions of functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can compose functions in either order, simplify the result, and explain how order changes the meaning and output.

**4c. Find inverses of relations.**  
Proficiency means a student can reverse ordered pairs or solve algebraically for the inverse relation and represent the inverse correctly.

**4d. Verify that two relations are inverses by using compositions.**  
Proficiency means a student can compose a relation with its inverse candidate and use the identity result to justify whether the pair are inverses.

**4e. Simplify expressions involving radicals and rational exponents.**  
Proficiency means a student can move between radical and rational-exponent notation, apply exponent rules, and simplify without changing value.

**4f. Simplify expressions in exponential or radical form.**  
Proficiency means a student can choose an efficient form, simplify expressions accurately, and explain the equivalence between forms.

**4g. Graph and analyze square root functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph square root functions with transformations, identify domain and range, and describe how the graph changes from the parent function.

**4h. Graph and analyze cube root functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph cube root functions, describe their symmetry and transformations, and compare them to square root graphs.

**4i. Simplify radical expressions.**  
Proficiency means a student can factor radicands, pull out perfect powers, and rewrite radicals in simplest form.

**4j. Add, subtract, and multiply radicals.**  
Proficiency means a student can combine like radicals, multiply radicals correctly, and simplify the results completely.

**4k. Divide and simplify radical expressions by rationalizing the denominator.**  
Proficiency means a student can remove radicals from denominators using appropriate algebraic methods and present the final expression in simplified form.

**4l. Solve radical equations in one variable and identify extraneous solutions.**  
Proficiency means a student can isolate radicals, solve the resulting equation, and check each candidate solution against the original equation.

**4m. Solve radical equations by graphing systems of equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can represent a radical equation as an intersection problem, use graphs to find solutions, and interpret why extraneous solutions may appear.

## Module 5: Exponential Functions and Geometric Series

**5a. Graph exponential growth functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph exponential growth models, identify initial value and growth factor, and describe how the graph changes over time.

**5b. Graph exponential decay functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph exponential decay models, identify decay factor and horizontal asymptote, and interpret the behavior in context.

**5c. Solve exponential equations in one variable.**  
Proficiency means a student can rewrite expressions with common bases or use logarithmic methods when needed to solve exponential equations accurately.

**5d. Solve exponential inequalities in one variable.**  
Proficiency means a student can solve exponential inequalities, preserve or reverse inequality logic appropriately, and express the solution set correctly.

**5e. Analyze expressions and functions involving the natural base `e`.**  
Proficiency means a student can interpret expressions involving `e`, evaluate them correctly, and explain why `e` is useful in growth contexts.

**5f. Generate geometric sequences.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify common ratios, write recursive or explicit rules, and generate terms of geometric sequences accurately.

**5g. Find sums of geometric series.**  
Proficiency means a student can use series formulas correctly, distinguish sequence from series, and compute finite geometric sums.

**5h. Choose the best function type to model sets of data.**  
Proficiency means a student can compare data patterns, choose an appropriate model family, and justify why that model is a better fit than alternatives.

## Module 6: Logarithmic Functions

**6a. Write logarithmic expressions in exponential form and write exponential expressions in logarithmic form.**  
Proficiency means a student can convert accurately between the two forms and explain how each statement expresses the same relationship.

**6b. Graph and analyze logarithmic functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph logarithmic functions, identify asymptotes and transformations, and describe domain, range, and key behavior.

**6c. Solve logarithmic equations using properties of equality.**  
Proficiency means a student can isolate logarithmic expressions, apply valid algebraic steps, and check that solutions satisfy domain constraints.

**6d. Simplify and evaluate expressions by using the properties of logarithms.**  
Proficiency means a student can apply product, quotient, and power rules correctly to expand, condense, or evaluate logarithmic expressions.

**6e. Solve exponential equations by using common logarithms.**  
Proficiency means a student can apply base-10 logarithms to solve equations when rewriting with a common base is not practical and interpret the result correctly.

**6f. Evaluate logarithmic expressions by using the Change of Base Formula.**  
Proficiency means a student can use change of base to evaluate logs on technology or by hand and explain why the method works.

**6g. Simplify expressions with natural logarithms.**  
Proficiency means a student can simplify `ln` expressions accurately and use properties of logarithms while respecting domains.

**6h. Solve exponential equations by using natural logarithms.**  
Proficiency means a student can apply `ln` to both sides of an equation when appropriate, solve for the variable, and interpret the exact or approximate solution.

**6i. Write and solve exponential growth equations and inequalities.**  
Proficiency means a student can model growth situations with exponential equations or inequalities, solve them, and explain what the result means in context.

**6j. Write and solve exponential decay equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can model decay situations correctly, solve for unknown quantities, and interpret decay behavior using the model parameters.

## Module 7: Rational Functions and Equations

**7a. Simplify rational expressions.**  
Proficiency means a student can factor numerator and denominator, reduce common factors appropriately, and identify excluded values from the original expression.

**7b. Simplify rational expressions by multiplying and dividing.**  
Proficiency means a student can multiply and divide rational expressions correctly, use reciprocal relationships when dividing, and simplify fully.

**7c. Simplify rational expressions by adding and subtracting.**  
Proficiency means a student can find common denominators, combine expressions accurately, and simplify the result without losing restrictions.

**7d. Simplify complex fractions.**  
Proficiency means a student can rewrite complex fractions using common denominators or division structure and simplify them to standard form.

**7e. Graph reciprocal functions by making tables of values.**  
Proficiency means a student can generate useful tables, plot reciprocal relationships accurately, and identify the resulting graph behavior.

**7f. Graph and write reciprocal functions by using transformations.**  
Proficiency means a student can write transformed reciprocal functions, identify shifts and stretches, and match equations to graphs.

**7g. Graph and analyze rational functions with vertical and horizontal asymptotes.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify asymptotes from the equation, sketch the graph accordingly, and describe end behavior and restrictions.

**7h. Graph and analyze rational functions with oblique asymptotes.**  
Proficiency means a student can determine slant asymptotes, use them to guide a sketch, and explain how the graph behaves near and far from the asymptote.

**7i. Recognize and solve direct and joint variation equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify direct and joint variation from words or equations, write a correct model, and solve for unknowns.

**7j. Recognize and solve inverse and combined variation equations.**  
Proficiency means a student can distinguish inverse and combined variation relationships, write the equation with the correct constant of variation, and solve accurately.

**7k. Solve rational equations in one variable.**  
Proficiency means a student can solve rational equations by clearing denominators or using equivalent transformations and reject extraneous solutions.

**7l. Solve rational inequalities in one variable.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify critical values, test intervals correctly, and express the solution set with appropriate exclusions.

## Module 8: Inferential Statistics

**8a. Classify sampling methods and identify bias in samples and survey questions.**  
Proficiency means a student can distinguish common sampling methods, identify potential sources of bias, and explain how sampling choices affect conclusions.

**8b. Distinguish among sample surveys, experiments, and observational studies.**  
Proficiency means a student can classify a study design accurately and explain what types of conclusions each design can reasonably support.

**8c. Compare theoretical and experimental probabilities.**  
Proficiency means a student can compute both theoretical and experimental probabilities and compare them to evaluate how closely data matches expectation.

**8d. Determine whether models are consistent with results from simulations of real-life situations.**  
Proficiency means a student can interpret simulation results, compare them to a proposed model, and decide whether the model is plausible based on evidence.

**8e. Describe distributions by finding their mean and standard deviation.**  
Proficiency means a student can compute or interpret mean and standard deviation and use those values to describe center and spread.

**8f. Classify variables and analyze probability distributions to determine expected outcomes.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify discrete and continuous variables, interpret a probability distribution, and use it to reason about likely outcomes.

**8g. Analyze normally distributed variables by using the Empirical Rule.**  
Proficiency means a student can use the 68-95-99.7 rule to estimate proportions within one, two, or three standard deviations of the mean.

**8h. Analyze standardized data and distributions by using z-values.**  
Proficiency means a student can compute and interpret z-scores and use them to compare values across distributions.

**8i. Use sample data to infer a population mean by using confidence intervals.**  
Proficiency means a student can interpret a confidence interval for a population mean and explain what the interval says about the likely range of the parameter.

**8j. Use sample data to infer a population proportion by using confidence intervals.**  
Proficiency means a student can interpret a confidence interval for a population proportion and relate the interval to a statistical claim about the population.

## Module 9: Trigonometric Functions

**9a. Draw angles in standard position and identify coterminal angles.**  
Proficiency means a student can place angles correctly in standard position, identify terminal sides, and generate coterminal angles in degrees or radians.

**9b. Convert between degree and radian measures and find arc lengths by using central angles.**  
Proficiency means a student can convert measures accurately and use the correct formulas to find arc length from radius and angle measure.

**9c. Find values of trigonometric functions for acute angles.**  
Proficiency means a student can use right-triangle ratios accurately and determine sine, cosine, and tangent values for acute angles.

**9d. Find values of trigonometric functions of general angles.**  
Proficiency means a student can determine trigonometric values for angles in any quadrant and use sign and reference-angle reasoning correctly.

**9e. Find values of trigonometric functions by using reference angles.**  
Proficiency means a student can identify a reference angle, choose the correct sign based on quadrant, and compute the target trigonometric value.

**9f. Find values of trigonometric functions given a point on a unit circle or the measure of a special angle.**  
Proficiency means a student can use unit-circle coordinates or special-angle facts to determine exact trigonometric values.

**9g. Find values of trigonometric functions that model periodic events.**  
Proficiency means a student can interpret periodic contexts, evaluate trig functions for specific inputs, and explain the meaning of the output.

**9h. Graph and analyze sine and cosine functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph sine and cosine functions, identify amplitude, period, and midline, and describe how those features shape the graph.

**9i. Model periodic real-world situations with sine and cosine functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can write a sine or cosine model for a periodic situation and interpret the parameters in context.

**9j. Graph and analyze tangent functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph tangent functions, identify period and asymptotes, and explain how the graph differs from sine and cosine.

**9k. Graph and analyze reciprocal trigonometric functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can graph reciprocal trig functions, identify asymptotes and domain restrictions, and relate them to sine, cosine, and tangent.

**9l. Graph horizontal translations of trigonometric functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can apply phase shifts correctly and show how horizontal translations change the graph of a trig function.

**9m. Graph vertical translations of trigonometric functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can shift trig graphs vertically, identify the new midline when appropriate, and describe the effect of the translation.

**9n. Find values of angle measures by using inverse trigonometric functions.**  
Proficiency means a student can use inverse trig functions to determine angle measures, work within principal-value ranges, and interpret the result in context.
